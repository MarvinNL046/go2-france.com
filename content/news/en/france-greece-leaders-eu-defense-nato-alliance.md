French President Emmanuel **Macron** and Greek Prime Minister Kyriakos Mitsotakis declared on April 26, 2026, in Athens that the European Union's expanding defense expenditures represent no viable alternative to the NATO alliance. This joint assertion, made during a bilateral meeting, emphasizes the enduring necessity of NATO for collective European defense, particularly in light of geopolitical challenges like Russia's invasion of Ukraine and tensions in the Indo-Pacific. The leaders' comments aim to clarify misconceptions about EU strategic autonomy while affirming transatlantic solidarity.

### Background on EU Defense Initiatives and NATO's Role

The European Union has ramped up its defense efforts in recent years, driven by the need to bolster collective security without relying solely on external partners. This "defense splurge," as described in reports, includes increased budgets for joint procurement, research, and capability development under frameworks like the European Defence Fund and Permanent Structured Cooperation (PESCO). France, a key proponent of EU strategic autonomy, has long advocated for greater European self-reliance in military matters, with **Macron** frequently calling for reduced dependence on non-EU actors.

Greece, as a frontline NATO member bordering volatile regions, shares concerns about regional stability but prioritizes the alliance's Article 5 collective defense guarantee. The two nations' leaders met amid broader discussions on Mediterranean security, energy diversification, and migration. Their statement counters narratives suggesting the EU could fully supplant NATO, especially as some EU members debate the alliance's future under varying U.S. administrations. According to AP News, the remarks underscore that EU initiatives complement rather than compete with NATO structures.

This position aligns with widely recognized public knowledge of NATO's foundational role since 1949, where it has served as the cornerstone of Euro-Atlantic security. The alliance currently comprises 32 members, including all EU states except for a few neutrals, and coordinates responses to threats like hybrid warfare and cyber attacks. France's return to NATO's integrated military command in 2009 further illustrates the intertwined nature of these organizations.

### Implications for European Security and Stability

The leaders' affirmation reinforces NATO's irreplaceability at a time when global uncertainties persist. Russia's ongoing aggression in Ukraine has prompted NATO to enhance its eastern flank defenses, with France and Greece contributing troops and capabilities. Greece, hosting key NATO facilities, plays a pivotal role in southeastern Europe, while France leads initiatives like the European Intervention Initiative to bridge gaps in rapid response forces.

For the EU, the defense push addresses historical underinvestment, where member states' spending often fell short of NATO's 2% GDP target. However, the statement clarifies that these efforts enhance interoperability within NATO rather than creating a parallel system. This balanced approach mitigates risks of division within Europe, ensuring unified deterrence against potential adversaries.
